Introduction
Beyond Post-its is a flexible, powerful toolkit
for creative problem-solving. You can use it any
time you want to structure a design thinking or
innovation session.
Every card in this toolkit has a second side —
your AI Extension — packed with ready-to-use
prompts for ChatGPT (or your preferred AI tool).
Why use them?
Go further, faster — AI can instantly expand
ideas, generate variations, and offer new
perspectives.
Unblock your thinking — Get fresh angles
when you’re stuck.
Save prep time — Prompts are pre-written for
each tool.

How to use the cards
Combine hands-on tools with AI to accelerate
creativity and clarity. Use them in any session
that blends physical collaboration with AIpowered thinking.
How to use:
Use the Card Front during your workshop to
guide the group’s activity.
Flip to the AI Extensions on the Card Back to
access additional prompts and variations.
Scan the QR code to instantly open a pre-written
AI prompt in ChatGPT (or your chosen AI tool).
Use AI-generated outputs as inspiration — then
refine, adapt, or combine them with the team’s
ideas.

Guide for Facilitators – Explore Stage
Expanding horizons and framing the right challenge
The Explore Stage is about opening up the problem space. This
is where teams broaden their perspective—understanding
users, contexts, and challenges in depth—before narrowing
down. It’s about seeing differently, asking better questions, and
discovering fresh opportunities.
Flow Overview
Frame the Challenge – set direction
Conduct Research – gather data
Build Empathy – see through users’ eyes
Analyse & Synthesise – make meaning
Define Problems – focus the challenge
Define Opportunities – shape where to play
Facilitator Reminders:
•Stay divergent first – let teams go wide before narrowing
down.
•People first – keep the focus on users, not assumptions.
•Make it visible – use maps, models, and diagrams to
create shared understanding.
•Frame well – a sharp problem or opportunity statement
is half the solution.

Facilitator Playbook – Explore Stage

3. Build Empathy
•

•
Immerse Yourself – experience the user’s world directly.
•
Observe Users – watch real behaviour, not just reported
stories.

Build a Persona – humanise insights with archetypes.

•

•
Draw Insights – extract meaning from patterns.
5. Define Problems

6. Define Opportunities
•
Map Opportunities – link insights to potential value.

The team has just received a challenge, a task,
a problem to address. A good understanding
of the complexity of the task is the first step to
success. A good discussion at this stage aligns
the team round the purpose.

discussion:

• What is some of the context of the challenge?
• What is the ultimate impact if you solve it?
• What constraints will you have while
addressing the brief (business, design,
technology, regulation)?

Problem Statement
You looked at the data, clustered them, found
patterns. Time to frame and re-frame the
problems. This is a key moment in your design
process. Here are some tips how to lead the team
through this activity:
❶ Stand back and ask the question: What does
our user really need? (Problem statement is
about Needs not Objects/Features)
❷ Write and re-write the statement until it really
captures the real problem you uncovered.
A good framing should have both the need and
your insight, e.g “She needs to communicate
the value of design, so that the business
understands and embraces it.”

the Solution
Reverse Solutions flips the problem on its
head: instead of asking “How do we solve
this?”, the team explores “How could we make
this problem worse?”
This playful inversion lowers the pressure to
be right, sparks laughter, and often surfaces
hidden assumptions. Once the “bad” solutions
are on the table, flipping them back around
reveals fresh, unconventional ideas that would
otherwise stay hidden.
❶ Start with “ how could we make it worse?”
❷ Brainstorm as many answers to the questions posed as possible.
❸ After generating “bad” or “reverse” solutions, ask the team: What’s the opposite of
this? How could this actually help us?
❹ Assess your solutions. Are any of the ideas
viable?

Frame Hypotheses
HYPOTHESIS is an instrument you use to prove
or refute your assumptions. You have identified
key assumptions. Frame them as Hypotheses.
The hypothesis includes 3 key elements:
1. The TARGET CUSTOMER
2. The FEATURE/SOLUTION you need to test
3. The VALIDATION, the benefit it will bring to
the user, the business and the society.
Here’s an example:
We believe that: (Target customer) Coffee drinkers on
the go, will find (Feature/Solution) recycling pods easy
and convenient to use, (Validation) enabling us to collect
good quality cups to recycle, increasing recycling rates by
20%.
A well written Hypothesis is Human Centric, Explicit, and Testable.
